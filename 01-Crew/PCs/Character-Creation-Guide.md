# Character Creation Guide — *The Shadow Exchange*

You're going to make a Blades in the Dark character before our first session together. This guide walks you through it. Read [`00-Canon/Player-Reference.md`](../../00-Canon/Player-Reference.md) first if you haven't — it has the system primer and the world primer in two pages.

When you're done, send your sheet to the GM. He needs your details to prep the world to fit you.

---

## Before you start

You are not making "a character who hunts monsters." You are making **one of four people who run a small, underfunded monster-hunting firm called Curios & Chronicles, fronted by a bookstore in a city where the supernatural is real and the public doesn't know it.** The work is professional and morally grey. The pay is mediocre. The tools are improvised. You are good at this in the way that people who do hard quiet work for years are good at it — not the way an action-movie hero is good at it.

This is the second year of your firm's existence, not the first day. Your character has already done some of this work. They have stories they don't tell.

---

## Step 1 — Pick a playbook

Blades has eight playbooks. From a D&D / Pathfinder lens, here's how they roughly land:

| Playbook | D&D analogue | Best for someone who… |
|---|---|---|
| **Hound** | Ranger | Likes ranged combat, tracking, tactical positioning. |
| **Whisper** | Warlock / Wizard | Wants to engage with the supernatural directly — ghosts, rituals, the boundary between worlds. |
| **Cutter** | Fighter / Barbarian | Likes physical confrontation. *Caution: Blades isn't HP-attrition combat. Don't pick Cutter expecting to "tank" — pick it because you like being the threat in the room.* |
| **Lurk** | Rogue | Stealth, climbing, cracking locks, getting in places you shouldn't. |
| **Slide** | Bard / Face | Social pressure, manipulation, lying with your whole chest. |
| **Spider** | Mastermind / Inquisitive | Loves planning, reading people, running schemes. **Strong choice for one player at the table — Spiders give the crew structure.** |
| **Leech** | Artificer | Tinkers with gadgets, alchemy, weird tech. **Excellent fit for our Containment Suite.** |
| **Slide / Spider / Leech** | — | Best onboarding picks for a Blades-first table. |

**Strong recommendation for the table as a whole:** at least one Spider or one Slide. Both have abilities that engage the crew with the world's politics rather than just the dice. If three people pick Cutters, the campaign will fight a lot and miss a lot.

If you're stuck, ask the GM for a recommendation — he knows what the rest of the table is picking and can tell you what slot is open.

---

## Step 2 — Name, look, heritage, background, vice

Each playbook has lists you can pick from, or invent your own. The categories:

- **Name & alias** — what you're called, and what you call yourself when working.
- **Look** — three details. Reach for: weather (damp wool coat), posture (stooped), and a tell (never makes eye contact when answering directly). Less is more.
- **Heritage** — where you're from. The campaign is a modern hidden-supernatural setting; pick a place that grounds your character in the world's texture.
- **Background** — what you did before this. *Academic, Labor, Law, Trade, Military, Noble* — pick one and write a one-line specific job. ("Pharmacist for an underfunded clinic." "Forensic accountant for a small firm." "Bartender at a place near a bus station.")
- **Vice & Purveyor** — what unhealthy thing you indulge in to clear stress, and the specific person/place who supplies it. **Pick a vice you find interesting to play, not just one that sounds cool.** You'll be doing this every downtime. *(Faith, Gambling, Luxury, Obligation, Pleasure, Stupor, Weird.)*

---

## Step 3 — Action ratings (your stats)

You have three attributes (Insight, Prowess, Resolve), each with four actions:

- **Insight** — Hunt, Study, Survey, Tinker
- **Prowess** — Finesse, Prowl, Skirmish, Wreck
- **Resolve** — Attune, Command, Consort, Sway

Starting array per the SRD:

- 7 dots to distribute among actions.
- Max 2 dots in any one action at start.
- At least one dot must go in each attribute.
- Then your playbook gives you one extra dot in a starting action.

**Practical advice for ex-D&D players:**

- *Don't optimize for one big number.* Blades pays you back for *breadth* (you can do many things competently) more than *spike* (you crit at one thing). 1-dot actions still roll one die.
- *Cover at least one of each attribute.* You'll be asked to resist consequences with whatever attribute matches the consequence type — having 0 in any attribute means resistance rolls there are dangerous.
- *Look at the attribute totals.* Your dots in an attribute also affect your *stress recovery* when you indulge vice. Spread enough that all three attributes have at least 1 dot.

---

## Step 4 — One starting special ability

Each playbook lists 8–9 special abilities. Pick **one**. (You'll earn more through XP.)

A few rules of thumb:

- **Read the ability twice.** The first read is the flavor; the second tells you when it actually triggers in play.
- **Pick something that gives you a *new option*, not a *bigger number*.** A +1d ability is fine. An ability that lets you do something you couldn't otherwise do is more interesting at the table.
- **If your playbook has a "veteran" ability that costs you one of your starting picks (cross-playbook), don't take it at character creation.** Save that for later.

---

## Step 5 — Friends & Rivals

Your playbook lists friends. **Pick one as a Close Friend** (someone who'll help you, with strings) and **one as a Rival** (someone who'll get in your way, with leverage).

Both will appear in play within the first three sessions. Tell the GM:

- Your Close Friend's name (if the playbook gives you a list, use a name from there or invent one).
- One sentence about why this person owes you, or you owe them.
- Your Rival's name and one sentence about why the friction.

These are not optional roleplay bonuses. They are how the GM knows what NPCs to put on stage in the first month.

---

## Step 6 — Items & Load

Don't build a packing list yet. In Blades, **you choose what you brought *during* the heist** — your "load" rating just tells you how many slots you have access to. Heavier load = more visible threat = more Heat.

Look at your playbook's item list. Mark the ones you find interesting — those are the things your character is "drawn toward" and reaches for first when planning a score. You don't have to commit to specific items now.

---

## Step 7 — The Cursed Family Heirloom

This is a *Shadow Exchange* prompt — not standard Blades. It's the most important thing on this list.

> **Question for you to answer:** *What object did you carry into this life that you should have left behind?*

Write down what it is. One sentence. Something concrete and specific — a pocket watch, a photograph, a piece of clothing, a journal in a language you don't speak. Something with weight from someone's life.

You don't need to invent backstory for it now. Just the object. The GM will quietly do something with it as the campaign goes on.

---

## Step 8 — One question for the GM

Pick one of these to ask the GM (or invent your own) and send the answer with your sheet:

- *What does my character think the work is for?*
- *Why is my character still doing this work?*
- *What does my character want, in plain language, that this work might give them?*

Your answer becomes part of how the GM weights what happens to your character. Don't write a paragraph. One sentence.

---

## What to send the GM

Before Session 0:

1. Filled-out playbook sheet (digital or photo of paper is fine).
2. Friends/Rivals — names + one sentence each.
3. Cursed Family Heirloom — the object.
4. The answer to your one question for the GM.

That's enough. Everything else, we figure out at the table.

---

## A few last notes

**You are not your character.** The system rewards taking damage, taking trauma, taking complicated moral hits. Don't play to "win" — play to make the story interesting. Your character's worst moments are where the campaign earns its weight.

**You don't have to know the rules to play.** The GM and the cheat sheets handle that. Your job is to know your character and make decisions that feel right for them. Everything else, you'll learn by doing.

**Ask questions.** If you're not sure what something on the sheet means, ask. Blades is full of small mechanics that look weird until they click.

See you at Session 0.
