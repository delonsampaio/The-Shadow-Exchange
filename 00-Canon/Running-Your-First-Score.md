# Running Your First Score — A Walkthrough

This document is written for someone who has never run a Forged in the Dark game. The cheat sheet has the quick reference. This has the *patterns* — how a Blades score actually flows at the table, with worked examples from Heist 1 (the Barghest at the theater).

Read this once before Session 1. You won't remember every line, and you don't need to — the cheat sheet handles fast recall. What you need from this document is a feel for *how the game wants to be run*.

---

## The macro shape of a session

Every Blades session has the same shape:

1. **Free play** — short scenes at the bookshop, on the street, between PCs. 10–20 minutes typically.
2. **The score** — engagement roll, then heist play. 60–90 minutes.
3. **Downtime** — vice, downtime activities, advancing personal projects. 30–45 minutes.

That's it. **Free play → Score → Downtime.** That rhythm is the whole game. If you find yourself spending 90 minutes in free play, you've drifted. If your downtime is one sentence ("you all rest"), you've also drifted. The rhythm only works if all three blocks get their time.

For Session 1 specifically, the rhythm is:

- Free play in the bookshop, the courier arrives, brief planning (10 minutes).
- Engagement roll, heist play (60–75 minutes).
- Tutorial downtime — slow, walked-through (30–40 minutes).

---

## The engagement roll — the most important roll of the score

In D&D, the players plan exhaustively before going through the door. **In Blades, you skip planning by calling the engagement roll.** Here is exactly how it works:

1. **The crew picks a plan type** (assault, deception, infiltration, occult, social, transport).
2. **They state a detail** — the *one* thing they need to fill in to make the plan concrete. *("We're walking in pretending to be Ministry inspectors, the detail is that we forged a recent inspection notice.")*
3. **You set the position** based on how exposed the plan is.
4. **You set the dice pool** based on factors:
   - +1d if their plan exploits a weakness.
   - +1d if they have a major advantage of surprise / preparation / scale.
   - −1d if it's bold or daring.
   - −1d if a faction is actively interfering.
5. **They roll the pool. Highest die determines starting position:**
   - **6:** start in **Controlled** position.
   - **4–5:** start in **Risky** position.
   - **1–3:** start in **Desperate** position. *(Often: a complication opens the score — a guard sees them, an alarm trips, the plan fails before it starts.)*
   - **Crit:** start with bonus effect or a free advantage.

Then **cut to the action.** Skip the entry. The crew is *already* inside the building, *already* in the alley, *already* talking to the guard. Whatever planning was needed, they did during downtime. If they need something specific, they flash back to having prepared it.

**This will feel wrong to a trad GM at first.** You'll want to play out the entry. Don't. Cut.

---

## The action roll — how every other roll in the game works

Once the score is underway, almost every player action that has interesting risk gets an action roll. Here's the ritual:

1. **The player describes what their character is doing.** *("I move along the catwalk to get a sight line on the Barghest.")*
2. **You name the action being rolled.** *("That's Prowl.")* If they want a different action, hear them out — fictional positioning is real. ("If they're going slow and methodical it could be Survey instead.")
3. **You set Position and Effect** out loud:
   - **Position** is how bad the consequences will be: Controlled / Risky / Desperate.
   - **Effect** is how much they accomplish: Limited / Standard / Great.
   - *("Risky / Standard — the catwalk creaks, but if you make it across you have a clear shot.")*
4. **The player can negotiate.** Maybe they want better effect by accepting worse position, or vice versa. Maybe they spend stress to push for +1d. Listen.
5. **They roll.** Number of d6 = their action rating. Highest die determines the result:
   - **1–3:** **Bad outcome.** They don't accomplish what they wanted, AND there's a consequence (often: position worsens for next roll, harm, or a clock fills).
   - **4–5:** **Mixed success.** They accomplish it (with reduced effect, or at a cost).
   - **6:** **Full success.** What they wanted.
   - **Two 6s:** **Critical.** Bonus on top.

**The math you need to internalize:** with 2 dice in the pool, players get a 6 about 30% of the time. With 3 dice, ~42%. **Mixed results (4–5) are the most common outcome at the table.** Most rolls produce *both* an outcome AND a consequence. That's the point.

---

## The consequence menu

When a roll is mixed (4–5) or bad (1–3), you owe the table a consequence. Here's the menu, ranked roughly by severity:

1. **Reduced effect** — they get *some* of what they wanted, not all.
2. **Complication** — a problem appears. *(A clock fills. A guard arrives. An NPC notices. The Detection Clock ticks.)*
3. **Worse position** — Risky becomes Desperate for the next action.
4. **Lost opportunity** — that approach is closed. They have to try the other plan.
5. **Harm** — physical or mental damage.

**Default for mixed:** complication.
**Default for bad:** complication + worse position.

Don't reach for harm first. Harm is dramatic and slow-moving. *Complications* are the campaign's heartbeat — they fill clocks, they raise stakes, they introduce twists.

**For Shadow Exchange specifically:** complications about the **cover-up** are scarier than damage. A witness with a phone is worse than a wound, because the cover-up is sacred. Reach for exposure consequences often.

---

## Resistance — the safety valve

When a consequence lands and a player doesn't like it, they can resist. **Always offer this in early sessions.**

> *"You can resist that. Roll [Insight if it's surprise, Prowess if it's physical, Resolve if it's mental or supernatural]. You'll spend 6 minus your highest die in stress. Do you want to?"*

If they say yes:
- They roll the resistance attribute.
- Spend stress = 6 minus highest die. *(Critical = clear 1 stress instead.)*
- The consequence is **reduced or negated entirely** — your call as GM, depending on what feels right in fiction.

**This is the system's pressure-release valve.** First-time players will not ask for resistance on their own. Prompt them. Three sessions in, they will start asking themselves.

---

## Flashbacks — the system's strangest superpower

A flashback retroactively establishes that a PC did some prep before the score. Players invoke it; you adjudicate cost.

**Cost in stress:**

- **0 stress** — something obviously plausible they would have done. *("I want to flash back to having an extra lead-lined bag.")*
- **1 stress** — Risky-equivalent prep. *("I flash back to having bribed the theater's night janitor for the basement key.")*
- **2 stress** — Desperate-equivalent prep. *("I flash back to having broken into the Ministry filing room last week to grab the building blueprints.")*

If the flashback requires a roll (the bribe might fail, the break-in is dangerous), they roll *now* — but at the time of the action, the prep already happened. The flashback never undoes a consequence already in play; it adds new fiction that helps with what's happening *next*.

**Invite flashbacks aggressively in Session 1 and 2.** Lines:

- *"Want to flash back to something you did during downtime that helps here?"*
- *"You can flash back to having grabbed [thing] — take 1 stress and tell me what you did."*
- *"This feels like a Spider's setup. Anyone been working on this?"*

Three sessions in, players invent flashbacks on their own. Until then, you carry the load.

## Worked example — running Heist 1

Let's walk through a hypothetical Heist 1 the way it actually plays at the table.

### Free play (10 minutes)

Open with the bookshop scene, the courier, the envelope, the Barghest job. Let the players talk about their plan for ten minutes. Don't overrule. Listen for who's reaching for what action — that tells you who the engagement roll position should favor.

### Engagement roll

Players settle on **infiltration** — break into the basement after hours, prowl past Merton, take the Adrenal Gland from him as he transforms.

**Detail:** they have a Lurk who flashed back during planning to having scouted the theater the night before. *(That's 1 stress, well-spent.)*

**Pool:**
- Standard 1d for infiltration plan.
- +1d for the scouting (preparation advantage).
- −1d for the Apex Aegis van being there (faction interference).

Net: 1d. They roll and get a 4.

**Result: Risky position to start.** You narrate them already inside the basement, lights off, the Barghest somewhere in the dark.

### Action 1 — finding Merton

The Hound says: *"I want to listen for him."*

You: *"That's Survey. Risky / Standard — if you spot him, you have the position; if you don't, you've already crossed the basement and he might spot you first."*

Hound rolls Survey 2 dice. Gets a 5.

**Mixed result.** You narrate: *"You hear the breathing — wet, wrong. He's behind the boiler. But there's a creak under your boot the moment you place your weight, and his breathing stops. He knows someone is here, he just doesn't know who."*

That's the consequence: Detection Clock ticks 1 of 4, and the Barghest's awareness has shifted.

### Action 2 — the shot

The Hound calls the shot: *"I'm taking him with the iron-and-salt round before he turns fully."*

You: *"Hunt, Risky / Standard. The salt is your weakness exploit, so I'll bump the effect to Great if you make the shot. But you're firing in the dark and he's behind cover."*

Player thinks about it. Spends 2 stress to push for +1d.

Rolls Hunt 3 dice. Gets a 6 and a 3.

**Full success.** You narrate the shot landing in the meat of his shoulder, the salt taking, the half-formed Barghest dropping to one knee. Now they have him *positioned* — and they have the moral choice.

### The moral pivot

The Cutter wants the Adrenal Gland. The Whisper has been reading Merton's locker via flashback (1 stress) and now describes the photograph: the woman, the children, the date.

You hold the moment. Let it land.

The crew decides. Whatever they decide, the Anchor Ledger gets an entry.

### Action 3 — extraction

The Leech wants the gland clean. *"I'm performing the extraction with the field kit."*

You: *"Physicking, Risky / Limited — you can do it, but the Adrenal Gland is fragile and the basement is not sterile. Or you can take it back to the lair on the table for Standard effect, but that doubles the time you're in the building."*

They choose to do it on-site. Roll Tinker 2 dice. Gets a 2.

**Bad outcome.** Narrate: *"The gland comes out, but you nicked the sac on the way. It's leaking — slowly. You have a window to get it cold. If you're not back at the lair within an hour, it's worthless."*

That's the consequence: a soft setup for the **Spoilage Clock** that you won't formally introduce until Session 3 or 4. You're seeding it.

### Cleanup and exit

Detection Clock is at 2 of 4. The Apex Aegis van is still outside.

You set up the exit with a quick Prowl roll for the team. They make it. Out the back, into the rain, lead-lined bag in hand, gland (hopefully) salvageable.

### Wrap-up

- **Coin:** Ministry payout per the bible (modest).
- **Double Bookkeeping:** if they hid the gland, prompt the Sway roll. Walk through it as a teaching moment.
- **Heat:** add Heat per the SRD's Heat table. Probably 2 — a clean job with one or two ticks of the clock.
- **Detection Clock:** reset.

End the score there. Cut to the bookshop, gland in cold storage. Now you're in downtime.

## Downtime — the engine of long-term play

After every score, every PC takes:

1. **Pay coin or take Heat** (lifestyle).
2. **Indulge Vice** — clear stress equal to the highest die rolled. Overindulge if highest die exceeds current stress. *(Overindulging is a roleplay opportunity, not a punishment — the player picks what trouble it causes.)*
3. **One downtime activity** (per Coin / Rep spent — extra activities cost 1 Coin or 2 Rep each):
   - **Acquire Asset** — temporary item or service.
   - **Long-Term Project** — work a clock toward a personal goal.
   - **Reduce Heat** — fictional cleanup, roll to reduce.
   - **Train** — XP advance.
   - **Recover** — Physick roll, advance harm-clearing clock.
4. **Entanglements** — roll if Heat is high enough. Often nothing in the early game.

**Tutorial walkthrough for Session 1's downtime:**

Walk through every step out loud. Have each player state:

- What their Vice scene looks like. *(One sentence — be evocative.)*
- What their downtime activity is. *(Encourage one Long-Term Project per PC — these become character throughlines.)*

Suggest first Long-Term Projects:

- Researching a Twice-Born seen at the theater entrance.
- Modifying a piece of Containment Suite gear for next score.
- Mending a Friend NPC relationship.
- Investigating the red-ink entry in the Hunter's Journal. *(This is a campaign hook — if a player picks this, the Architect thread starts moving.)*

## What you say most often as a Blades GM

If you only remember four lines, remember these:

1. **"What's your Position and Effect?"** *(Said before every roll.)*
2. **"You can resist that — want to?"** *(Said after every consequence.)*
3. **"Want to flash back to something?"** *(Said any time the players are stuck.)*
4. **"What does this cost the cover-up?"** *(Said when you need a complication for The Shadow Exchange specifically.)*

Everything else is variations on those four.

---

## The mistakes you will make

Every first-time Blades GM makes some version of these. Calling them out so you recognize them when they happen:

- **Letting planning run too long.** Cut to the engagement roll at 10 minutes. Trust the flashback system.
- **Forgetting to set Position/Effect out loud.** Every roll. Every time. Until it's habit.
- **Reaching for harm when complication would serve.** Harm slows the game. Complications accelerate it.
- **Forgetting to invite resistance.** Players will not ask for it. You must offer.
- **Treating clocks as decorative.** Tick them visibly. Players who can see the Detection Clock at 3 of 4 make different decisions than players who can't.
- **Keeping the world frozen between sessions.** Factions move. Clocks tick. Use the faction tracker and the active clocks file.
- **Trying to predetermine outcomes.** You cannot predict where a Blades score goes. Plan the *situation*, not the *story*. The story is what you and the players make at the table.
- **Forgetting downtime is half the game.** Don't shortchange it.

---

## When in doubt

- **"What's the most interesting consequence?"** — your default question for any mixed or bad result.
- **"What does the cover-up cost here?"** — your campaign-specific complication generator.
- **"Whose name is on the Anchor I'm about to take?"** — your moral-weight check.
- **"What does my player need next?"** — for pacing. Sometimes the answer is a win, sometimes a setback, sometimes just a quiet beat. Trust the rhythm.

When you're truly stuck: **ask the table.** *"What does this look like?"* is a perfectly valid question. Blades is a collaborative game — leaning on the table is a strength, not a weakness.

---

You're going to be fine. The first session will be rougher than you'd like. The second will click. By the third you'll feel it. The campaign earns its weight in the spaces between the rules — keep your eye on Anchors, on the cover-up, on the people whose lives the work touches. The system handles itself.
