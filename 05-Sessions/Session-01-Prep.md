> "It's raining. A steady, miserable drizzle streaking the perpetually dusty windows of Curios & Chronicles. Inside: old parchment, floor wax, tea on the hot plate. Out front it looks like a bookstore that makes twenty dollars a day — exactly what you want.

>
> Inside the envelope: a grainy photograph of a local politician's dog — except the dog has two human hands where its front paws should be. **GM prompt to the table:** "The Ministry expects you to kill the thing and torch the body. But an Ethereal Adrenal Gland from a Barghest fetches serious money on the black market. Who is prepping gear in the Containment Suite, and what is our plan for getting into the theater?"

Let the planning go for ten minutes, no more. Then call the Engagement roll.

## Heist 1 — The Standard Job

### The Target

A Barghest living in the basement of a local theater.

### Three Layers — Merton

1. **Appears as:** night security at the theater for eleven years. Has a dog. Locker contains a keychain photo — a woman and two children, long dead.
2. **Actually is:** a Barghest. Anchor Item is a worn leather dog collar — his first dog, 1823.
3. **Became this because:** summoned without consent in 1823. Has been fighting what he is every night since. The summoner is long dead. *(Available if players investigate before acting.)*

**Holy Weakness:** salt packed into wounds; an iron bell struck at the precise moment of midnight.

### Goals

- **Ministry goal:** kill it, burn the body, file an incident report citing a gas main issue.
- **Crew goal:** extract the Ethereal Adrenal Gland cleanly (Finesse or Physicking), hide it in a lead-lined bag, get out before Ministry cleanup arrives.

### Engagement-roll setup

Three viable approaches, each with a default Position. Let the players pick — or invent a fourth. 
*(GM: Use the read-alouds below to teach them what the roll result actually means in fiction!)*

**1. Front-door professional cleanup.** Walk in with a ladder and a Ministry vest. Quick, public, honest-looking.
- Position / Effect: Risky / Standard.
- Complication on 1–3: a janitor sees something. The cover-up risk arrives before the monster does.

**2. After-hours break-in via the alley.** Slow, surgical.
- Position / Effect: Controlled / Standard if Prowl-aspected; Risky / Standard otherwise.
- Complication on 1–3: the Apex Aegis van's occupants are *also* watching the alley. The crew is being filmed.

**3. Talk to Merton before fighting him.** Either by accident or by design.
- Position / Effect: Risky / Limited *(he doesn't want to talk; he is barely holding it together)*.
- Complication on 1–3: he reaches for someone he loved instead of the crew, and the moment breaks.

> **IF THEY ROLL A 6 (Controlled Start):**
> "The intel was flawless. The Ministry patrol just rounded the far corner, the heavy iron gate is unlocked exactly as promised, and you are standing in the blind spot of the cameras. You have the breathing room you paid for. What do you do?"

> **IF THEY ROLL A 4-5 (Risky Start):**
> "You’re in, but the world isn't cooperating. You slip through the maintenance door, but the rusted hinges screech loud enough to echo. Above you, on the catwalk, a flashlight beam snaps on and starts sweeping down toward your position. You have seconds before you are spotted. What do you do?"

> **IF THEY ROLL A 1-3 (Desperate Start):**
> "The plan didn't survive contact with reality. You kick open the basement door expecting an empty room, but the target is fully awake, staring right at you, and the Apex Aegis drone outside just locked onto your heat signature. It is going wrong, right now. What do you do?"

### Mid-scene read-alouds

Drop these as the score crosses thresholds. They are not boxed text in the bible — they are mine, written for first-time Blades players who need the sensory detail labeled.

> **The theater after hours, before descent:**
>
> "The auditorium is the kind of dark that makes you turn your head trying to triangulate sound. Stale popcorn grease in the carpet. Worklights still on along the apron of the stage. A wedding photo from 1996 in the manager's office that no one has thought about in years. The basement door is propped open with a brick. Someone goes down there every night and the someone has not come back up yet."

> **Merton's locker, if they search it:**
>
> "The padlock isn't even closed. Inside: a pressed work shirt on a wire hanger, a thermos that smells like instant coffee, a cardboard box of dog biscuits half-emptied, a keychain photograph behind a layer of plastic gone yellow with age. A woman and two children. The shirt collars, the hairstyles — you'd put them at 1820s. The photograph itself is impossible. Someone made it for him. Someone who knew exactly what he wanted to hold onto."

> **First sight of Merton in his true form:**
>
> "What he is now is taller than what he was. The shape suggests a dog the way a wax sculpture suggests a face — close enough to know what it should be, wrong in the parts that matter. He is not coming at you. He is standing very still in the basement light, breathing through teeth that no longer fit a mouth, and he is looking at the dog collar in his hand like he has never seen it before."

### GM REMINDER: Teaching Combat and the Devil's Bargain
Before they engage Merton, remind the trad players: **There is no initiative.** Do not roll for turn order. Ask: *"Who is acting first?"* and resolve it as a single action roll. 

On the very first combat roll, **offer the Devil's Bargain:**
> *"I will give you an extra die to make this shot. But the world takes its cut. If you take this die, the Ministry scanner on the corner logs your weapon's discharge. It happens if you hit; it happens if you miss. Do we have a deal?"*

If they take a bad consequence from Merton, explicitly offer the Resistance read-aloud:
> *"The complication lands. The Barghest's claws tear through your armor. But you do not have to accept that reality. You can spend your own Stress to dig your heels in and rewrite what just happened. Do you take the hit, or do you resist?"*

### Anchor Item — Merton's collar

Worn leather dog collar. His first dog, 1823. Ministry protocol = seize and destroy.

If destroyed, his loss of control accelerates significantly. *(GM: this is information available if they investigate; don't volunteer it. If they ask the trophy wall journal, give it.)*

If seized intact and stored in the Lead-Lined Vault: his disguise ends in 1d6 hours, and he has nowhere to go. He will be a tragedy the Ledger has to record.

If left where he can reach it: he reverts on his own timeline, and the Ministry calls the cleanup a botched job.

**Log whatever they decide in `08-Loot-and-Anchors/Anchor-Ledger.md`.**

## Tutorial downtime — walk it through

After the score, take a deliberate 30–40 minutes to teach the Downtime cycle. Do not rush it. The four activities are the engine of long-term play.

For each PC, walk through:

1. **Pay coin or take Heat?** Quick reminder of the heat phase.
2. **Indulge Vice.** Let them describe the scene briefly. This is the first place character flavor lives.
3. **One downtime activity each:**
   - **Acquire Asset** — what one thing do they need that they don't have?
   - **Long-Term Project** — start one. Suggested first projects: research on a Twice-Born seen at the theater entrance, modifying a piece of equipment for the next score, mending the relationship with a Friend NPC.
   - **Reduce Heat** — frame as fictional, not just dice.
   - **Train** — XP advance.
4. **Entanglements?** Roll once, narrate it small if anything triggers. Most likely nothing this early.

### Downtime montage read-aloud

> **READ ALOUD — over the downtime:**
>
> "A week passes. The bookshop opens at the same time every morning and closes at the same time every night and Consequence the cat continues to ignore everyone equally. The gland is in cold storage. The collar is wherever you put it. The Ministry has paid you and stopped thinking about you. Somewhere across the city, an Apex Aegis analyst has filed a memo about a small firm operating in their territory and the memo has been read by exactly one person. You don't know that yet."

This is also where you plant **one Omen Clock symptom in the periphery**, without ticking the clock yet. Examples:

- Consequence the cat refuses to enter the back room for three days. Then resumes normally.
- The Scanner Station picks up a single AM Host fragment about "something patient under the city" between bursts of static, then nothing.
- The streetlight outside the bookshop has been out for a week. The other lights on the block are fine.

Pick one. Do not explain it. Do not advance the clock. The pattern needs three before it means anything.
